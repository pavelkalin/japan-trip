Tokyo 東京 6 nights

The world's largest metropolitan area: 37 million people, the busiest train station on Earth (Shinjuku, 3.6 million riders daily), and a city that reinvents itself every decade. Base yourself in Shinjuku, Shibuya, or near Ueno. Day 1 is a soft jet-lag day — don't overplan it.

Shinjuku 新宿

Tokyo's neon-soaked downtown — towering skyscrapers next to centuries-old ramen alleys.

📖 The story

Shinjuku Station handles 3.6 million passengers a day — about 40% of Israel's population, passing through one building. After WW2 destroyed most of the area, it became Tokyo's "second downtown," explicitly designed as the city's western commercial hub. The west side (skyscrapers) is corporate Tokyo; the east side (Kabukicho, Golden Gai) is its wilder mirror.

👀 Don't miss
  • Omoide Yokocho ("Memory Lane") — 60+ tiny post-WW2 ramen and yakitori stalls in a 4-meter-wide alley
  • Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building — free observation deck at 202m, often less crowded than Skytree
  • Godzilla Head peeking over the Toho Cinemas building in Kabukicho
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Take the kids to the free Tokyo Government observation deck before dinner — they'll see how vast Tokyo really is. On clear evenings you can see Mt. Fuji silhouetted at sunset.

🍜 Eat here

Omoide Yokocho for atmosphere — try Asadachi for grilled chicken or Iseya for yakitori. For family-friendly: Ichiran Ramen (individual booth seating, kids love it) or any of the floors of Lumine Est department store.

Sensō-ji 浅草寺

Tokyo's oldest temple, founded in 645 AD around a tiny golden Kannon statue that two fisherman brothers pulled from the Sumida River.

📖 The story

In 628 AD, two fisherman brothers, Hinokuma Hamanari and Hinokuma Takenari, caught a small golden statue of Kannon (the bodhisattva of compassion) in their fishing net. Their village headman converted his house into a small temple. By 645 AD the temple had grown into Sensō-ji. Over 14 centuries it became the spiritual heart of old Edo — destroyed in WW2 firebombings and rebuilt in concrete in 1958, faithful in every detail except material.

👀 Look for
  • The 700kg paper lantern at Kaminarimon ("Thunder Gate") — look up at the underside for the carved dragon
  • The five-story pagoda on the left side of the main hall — dramatically lit at night
  • Asakusa Shrine behind the main hall — same complex, near-empty, dedicated to the fishermen brothers
  • Incense cauldron in front of the temple — bathe yourself in the smoke for health
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The omikuji fortune ritual is hands-on magic: shake the silver box, pull a numbered stick, find your fortune in the drawer. 100 yen each. If you get bad fortune, tie it to the metal rack so it stays at the temple.

🍜 Eat here

Ningyo-yaki — sponge cakes shaped like little dolls, filled with sweet red bean, baked fresh on Nakamise-dori. Asakusa Kagetsudo sells what's widely considered Tokyo's best melon-pan. For lunch: Daikokuya tempura (since 1887).

📸 Photo spot

The Asakusa Culture Tourist Information Center across from Kaminarimon has a free 8th-floor observation deck — view of the temple, Nakamise, and Skytree in one frame.

⏱ 2–3 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 Asakusa Station 🕐 Grounds always open

Nakamise-dori 仲見世通り

A 250m approach to Sensō-ji lined with 88 stalls — one of the world's oldest continually-operating shopping streets.

📖 The story

The shops here date back to the early 1700s, when locals were given permission to set up stalls in exchange for cleaning the temple grounds. Many current shopowners are direct descendants of the original families. Officially shops open 9am but many start earlier; closing time is around 7pm.

👀 Best stalls
  • Kibido — kibi-dango millet dumplings, made on the spot
  • Sukeroku — handmade traditional Japanese toys (Edo-period style)
  • Kuroda — handcrafted folding fans, since 1856
  • Tokiwado — kaminari-okoshi (puffed rice candy), the temple's signature snack
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Give each kid a budget (¥1000 / ~$7) and let them pick a souvenir from anywhere on the street. Best toy and candy variety of any tourist spot in Tokyo.

⏱ 1 hr 💰 Snacks ¥100–500 🚇 Asakusa Stn 🕐 9am–7pm

Tokyo Skytree 東京スカイツリー

At 634 meters, the world's tallest tower (and second-tallest free-standing structure on Earth after Burj Khalifa).

📖 The story

Opened in 2012 to replace Tokyo Tower as the city's digital TV broadcasting hub — Tokyo Tower wasn't tall enough to clear the new skyscrapers. The number 634 was chosen because in old Japanese it can be read "mu-sa-shi" — Musashi, the old name of this region. Designed to withstand a 7.0 earthquake using a central concrete shaft + traditional pagoda-inspired counter-vibration.

👀 Two observation decks
  • Tembo Deck (350m) — main viewing platform with glass floor sections
  • Tembo Galleria (450m) — spiral walkway tube going up another 100m
  • Solamachi mall at the base — 300+ shops, restaurants, an aquarium, and a planetarium
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The glass floor section on the Tembo Deck is a thrill (or terror) — stand on it and look straight down 340m. The Sumida Aquarium in Solamachi is great if anyone wants a break.

📸 Photo spot

For Mt. Fuji visible behind Tokyo, the Tembo Galleria at sunset on a clear winter/early-spring day. Best photo OF Skytree: the Jukkenbashi Bridge with the tree reflecting in the Kita-jukken river.

⏱ 2 hrs 💰 ¥3100/adult (~$22) 🚇 Oshiage Stn 🕐 8am–10pm 💡 Book online — skip lines

Shibuya Crossing 渋谷スクランブル交差点

The world's busiest pedestrian crossing — up to 3,000 people cross in a single light cycle, 2.4 million per day.

📖 The story

Five streets converge here in a "scramble" crossing where every direction stops for pedestrians at once. It became globally iconic via Lost in Translation (2003) and dozens of Hollywood films since. Across the street, the Hachiko statue commemorates the most loyal dog in Japanese history — an Akita who came to Shibuya Station every day for 9 years and 9 months waiting for his deceased owner Professor Ueno.

👀 Best viewpoints
  • Shibuya Sky (top of Scramble Square, opened 2019) — open-air rooftop deck with the best Tokyo skyline view, with Fuji on clear days
  • Starbucks Tsutaya — second-floor window, free with a coffee, classic photo spot
  • Mag's Park — rooftop of Shibuya 109 with crossing view, less touristy
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Cross it at night when neon billboards are at max. Then visit Hachiko — kids respond to the story. Shibuya Sky has a tilted glass observation rim where you can lie back and look at sky — kids will love the photo.

🍜 Eat here

Uobei sushi in Shibuya — conveyor sushi with iPad ordering, ¥130 per plate. The Bunkamura Dogenzaka area has good izakaya. For dessert: Magnet by Shibuya 109 for crepes.

⏱ 1–2 hrs 💰 Free (Sky ¥2500) 🚇 Shibuya Stn 🌙 Best at night

Harajuku & Takeshita Street 原宿

Tokyo's youth fashion epicenter since the 1970s — a 400m candy-colored alley your tweens will lose their minds over.

📖 The story

Takeshita Street became a teen-fashion mecca in the 1970s when boutiques selling foreign and avant-garde clothing first opened. By the 1990s–2000s it birthed the "Harajuku Girls" phenomenon — wild colorful fashion subcultures (Lolita, Decora, Gyaru). Today it's tamer but still the densest concentration of kawaii (cute) culture on Earth.

👀 Don't miss
  • Totti Candy Factory — rainbow cotton candy the size of a basketball
  • Daiso Harajuku — massive 100-yen shop
  • Kiddy Land on Omotesando — 5 floors of Japanese toys, Pokemon, Hello Kitty, Studio Ghibli
  • Cat Street — quieter back lane connecting Harajuku to Shibuya, edgier shops
  • Omotesando — the "Champs-Élysées of Tokyo," high fashion and architecture
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Your 9-year-old daughter will especially love this stretch. Plan for 2 hours minimum. Saturdays the street is shoulder-to-shoulder; go on a weekday morning.

🍜 Eat here

Crepes are the signature food: Marion Crepes (since 1976) or Santa Monica Crepes. For lunch: Afuri Ramen (yuzu-citrus broth, milder for kids). For instagram cuteness: Sweet XO Good Grief rainbow cheesecake.

⏱ 2 hrs 💰 Snacks ¥500–1500 🚇 Harajuku Stn ⚠️ Avoid weekends

Meiji Shrine 明治神宮

A sprawling forest shrine dedicated to Emperor Meiji — total tranquility just 100m from Harajuku's chaos.

📖 The story

Built in 1920 to honor Emperor Meiji (whose reign ended Japan's 250-year isolation and propelled it into the modern age) and Empress Shōken. Destroyed by US air raids in 1945, rebuilt 1958. The surrounding 70-hectare forest was planted from scratch — 100,000 trees donated from across Japan and Korea, designed by foresters to evolve into a self-sustaining "eternal" forest over 100 years. It now reads as ancient woodland in the center of Tokyo.

👀 Look for
  • Massive cypress torii gates — the wood is 1,500 years old, from Taiwan
  • Sake barrels — donated by every major sake brewer in Japan
  • French wine barrels facing them — from Burgundy, honoring Emperor Meiji's love of European wine
  • Wedding processions — Sunday mornings often have traditional Shinto weddings
  • Ema — wooden prayer plaques hung around the shrine, you can buy one and write a wish
🍜 Eat here

The shrine itself has nothing — eat in Harajuku before/after. For something close: Café Mori no Terrace just outside the main gate, blends into the forest.

⏱ 1.5 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 Harajuku Stn 🕐 Sunrise–sunset

Yoyogi Park 代々木公園

One of Tokyo's largest parks and the center of its hanami (cherry blossom viewing) culture.

📖 The story

The land here has had many lives — Imperial Japanese Army parade grounds, US Army housing after WW2, the Olympic Village for the 1964 Tokyo Olympics, and a park since 1967. 134 acres, 600+ cherry trees. On weekends it hosts everything from rockabilly dance circles to capoeira classes to amateur bands.

👀 Look for
  • Rockabilly dancers — at the park's main entrance Sunday afternoons
  • Cosplay gatherings — random Sundays, anime fans in elaborate costumes
  • NHK Hall & Tokyo Camii mosque nearby
🍜 Eat here

Picnic from a 7-Eleven or Lawson — onigiri rice balls, karaage fried chicken, and bento boxes are all under ¥500 and surprisingly excellent. This is what locals do for hanami.

📸 Photo spot

The central walkway under the cherry tunnel in full bloom is iconic. For something unique: the fountain plaza on the south end at golden hour.

⏱ 1–2 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 Harajuku/Yoyogi Stn 🌸 Peak hanami spot

Tokyo DisneySea 東京ディズニーシー

The only DisneySea park in the world — and consistently rated one of the best Disney parks anywhere.

📖 The story

Opened in 2001, designed entirely around water and exploration themes. Seven uniquely-themed "ports" rather than traditional Disney "lands." More mature and detailed than Disneyland — perfect for ages 9 and up, who'll appreciate the cinematic immersion. Fantasy Springs, a massive new area added in 2024, has Frozen, Tangled, and Peter Pan attractions.

👀 Best of the 7 ports
  • Mediterranean Harbor — entrance area, gondolas, Mediterranean town built on water
  • Mysterious Island — inside a 50m active volcano, Jules Verne theme
  • Mermaid Lagoon — indoor area, gentle rides, best for younger kids
  • Arabian Coast — Aladdin's world, Sinbad ride (underrated)
  • Lost River Delta — Aztec ruins, Indiana Jones ride, Tower of Terror nearby
  • Port Discovery — retro-future, Aquatopia and Soaring rides
  • Fantasy Springs (new 2024) — Frozen, Tangled, Peter Pan
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

For ages 9, 11, 12: prioritize Tower of Terror, Journey to the Center of the Earth, Soaring: Fantastic Flight, and the new Anna and Elsa's Frozen Journey. Buy Premier Access (Disney's paid line-skip, ~$15/ride) selectively for top picks to save 60–90min waits each.

🍜 Park snacks worth it

Tokyo Disney is famous for its themed food: alien mochi (gooey little aliens in Port Discovery), chandu tail shrimp dumplings in Arabian Coast, gyoza dog in American Waterfront. Get the popcorn refill bucket — flavors include curry, garlic shrimp, milk chocolate, jalapeño.

📸 Photo spot

Mediterranean Harbor at sunset, with Mt. Prometheus (the volcano) erupting in the background. The nightly water show ("Believe! Sea of Dreams") closes the park.

Hakone — Mt. Fuji views

Loading…

Hakone 箱根

A mountain hot-spring town 80 minutes from Tokyo, traversed by a circuit of trains, cable cars, ropeways, and pirate ships — all included in the Hakone Free Pass.

📖 The story

Hakone has been a major hot-spring resort since the 9th century, popular with samurai who came to soak after battles. Today it's where Tokyoites escape on weekends for onsen. The full Hakone Loop is a one-day adventure using 5 different modes of transport in a single circuit.

👀 The full loop (counterclockwise)
  • 1. Hakone-Yumoto — arrive by Odakyu Romancecar from Shinjuku (90 min)
  • 2. Hakone-Tozan switchback railway — zigzags up the mountainside, the only one of its kind in Japan
  • 3. Hakone Open-Air Museum (optional) — outdoor sculpture park with Picasso pavilion, kid-friendly play sculptures
  • 4. Cable car to Sounzan
  • 5. Hakone Ropeway to Owakudani — flies over a sulfur volcanic valley with steaming vents
  • 6. Owakudani — buy kuro-tamago (eggs boiled in sulfur water, turn black — supposedly each adds 7 years to your life)
  • 7. Ropeway down to Lake Ashi
  • 8. Pirate ship across Lake Ashi — full-scale reproduction galleon with Mt. Fuji views
  • 9. Hakone Shrine — the iconic red torii standing in the lake
  • 10. Bus back to Hakone-Yumoto, train back to Tokyo
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Five modes of transport in one day is a kids' dream — the pirate ship and ropeway in particular. The black eggs are unforgettable. If anyone gets tired, the Hakone Open-Air Museum has playable sculptures designed for kids.

📸 Photo spot

The iconic Hakone Shrine torii in Lake Ashi — the gate stands knee-deep in the water with Mt. Fuji behind. From Motohakone-ko port, walk south 5 minutes. Best in early morning.

⏱ Full day 💰 ¥6,100 Free Pass (~$43) 🚇 Shinjuku → Romancecar ☁️ Check Fuji forecast AM

Mt. Fuji 富士山

Japan's tallest mountain (3,776m) and one of the most photographed mountains on Earth.

📖 The story

An active stratovolcano, last erupted in 1707–1708. Sacred since prehistory — pilgrims have climbed it for over 1,000 years. The first non-Japanese to summit was British envoy Sir Rutherford Alcock in 1860. In late March/early April it's still fully snow-capped, a striking contrast to the cherry blossoms below.

👀 Best views
  • Lake Ashi (Hakone) — water + Fuji + Hakone Shrine torii
  • Chureito Pagoda (Fujiyoshida) — the iconic five-story pagoda with Fuji + sakura shot
  • Lake Kawaguchi — closest of the Fuji Five Lakes, mirror reflections
  • Shinkansen window — between Tokyo and Kyoto, around Shin-Fuji station (sit on the right going west)
⚠️ Fuji is shy

The mountain is visible only about 30% of days on average — best chance is early morning in winter and early spring. Check livecameras.jp the morning of your trip.

👀 Best Nov–April 🌅 Clearest at dawn 📍 Hakone vantage point

Ueno Park 上野公園

Tokyo's biggest cultural park and arguably its most famous hanami spot — 1,000+ cherry trees over 133 hectares.

📖 The story

The land was originally a temple complex (Kan'ei-ji) destroyed in the 1868 Battle of Ueno during the Boshin War, when imperial forces ended the samurai era. Cherry trees were planted in the 17th century specifically to attract people for hanami. Today it hosts the city's biggest sakura crowds.

👀 Inside the park
  • Tokyo National Museum — Japan's #1 museum, samurai armor and swords
  • National Museum of Western Art — UNESCO site, designed by Le Corbusier; Monets, Rodins
  • Ueno Zoo — Japan's oldest, home to giant pandas (Xiang Xiang line)
  • Shinobazu Pond — lotus-covered, with a small island shrine
  • Toshogu Shrine — gilded shrine to Tokugawa Ieyasu, often overlooked
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

If you're going to do one museum: the Tokyo National Museum's samurai gallery in the Honkan building is gold for kids ages 9+. The zoo is fine but Tokyo's zoo aesthetic is dated.

📸 Photo spot

The central Sakura-dori path in full bloom — a pink tunnel a quarter-mile long.

⏱ 2–3 hrs 💰 Park free; museums ¥1000+ 🚇 Ueno Stn 🌸 Peak hanami

teamLab Planets チームラボプラネッツ

Walk-through digital art museum where you wade barefoot through projected koi-filled water, get lost in infinity mirror rooms, and lie back in a sea of moving light.

📖 The story

teamLab is a Tokyo-based collective of programmers, animators, mathematicians, and designers. Planets (opened 2018, expanded 2023) is one of two teamLab venues in Tokyo — distinguished from teamLab Borderless by being more "immersive" (you walk through water, climb on things) rather than just visual.

👀 Best installations
  • Drawing on the Water Surface — wade thigh-deep through water with thousands of projected koi swimming around you, reacting to your movement
  • Floating Flower Garden — 13,000+ live orchids hang from the ceiling, lifting up as you walk under
  • Infinity Crystal Universe — mirror room with LED lights extending forever in all directions
  • Soft Black Hole — bouncy floor where you sink and roll
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

You'll get wet up to your knees — bring shorts or rolled-up pants. Lockers and towel rentals available. About 1.5 hours one-way through the venue. Book online 2+ weeks in advance — sells out fast.

📸 Photo spot

Phones encouraged. The Infinity Crystal Universe is the iconic Instagram shot. The orchid garden makes for soft portrait lighting.

Odaiba お台場

A futuristic artificial island in Tokyo Bay — built originally as 1850s coastal defense forts, today a sci-fi waterfront.

📖 The story

The "daiba" (forts) here were built in 1853 after Commodore Perry's "Black Ships" arrived demanding Japan open to trade. Today the island is reclaimed land hosting malls, museums, and Tokyo's most prominent geek landmark: a life-size Gundam.

👀 Don't miss
  • Unicorn Gundam Statue — life-size 19.7m statue outside DiverCity Plaza, transforms at 11am, 1pm, 3pm, 5pm + light show after dark
  • Rainbow Bridge — best skyline view from Odaiba Seaside Park
  • Miraikan (science museum) — hands-on, includes ASIMO demos and a 6m animated globe
  • Aqua City Statue of Liberty — yes, there's a Statue of Liberty replica here
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Time your visit to a Gundam transformation. Boys 9–12 will not believe what they're seeing. teamLab Planets is a 10-minute walk away — pair them.

Kyoto 京都 6 nights

The cultural soul of Japan. Capital from 794 to 1868 AD — 1,074 years. The city has 1,600 Buddhist temples, 400 Shinto shrines, 14 UNESCO World Heritage sites, and 5 active geisha districts. Slower than Tokyo, more walkable. Base around Gion or downtown Kawaramachi.

Gion 祇園

Kyoto's 400-year-old geisha district — wooden machiya houses, lantern-lit streets, and the slim chance of spotting a maiko hurrying between teahouses at dusk.

📖 The story

Gion started in the medieval period as a place for travelers to rest before visiting Yasaka Shrine. By the Edo period it had become Kyoto's premier entertainment district, with ochaya (teahouses) where geisha entertained samurai and merchants. Today about 180 geiko (Kyoto's term for geisha) and maiko (apprentices, age 15–20) still work here.

A maiko trains for 5 years before becoming a full geiko. The kimono, hair, makeup, and dance training is among the most rigorous artistic disciplines in the world.

👀 Walk this route
  • Hanami-koji — the iconic main street, lantern-lit lanes of ochaya
  • Shirakawa area — narrow stone bridges over the Shirakawa stream, dramatic willows
  • Kennin-ji — Kyoto's oldest Zen temple (1202), often nearly empty
  • Yasaka Shrine — at the east end, free and beautiful at night
⚠️ Etiquette

Strict rules now: no photographing geisha in private alleys; tourists were harassing maiko. Side streets off Hanami-koji are now off-limits to non-residents with ¥10,000 fines. Stick to Hanami-koji and main streets.

🍜 Eat here

Gion has Michelin-starred kaiseki ($300+/person, reservation only) but also good casual: Gion Kappa (yakitori), Gion Karyo (mid-range kaiseki). For tea + sweets: Tsujiri at the Hanami-koji corner, world-class matcha desserts.

⏱ 1–2 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 Gion-Shijo Stn 🌙 Best at dusk

Pontocho Alley 先斗町

A narrow lantern-lit alley between Kamogawa river and Kiyamachi street — Kyoto's most atmospheric dinner street.

📖 The story

Pontocho was established in 1670 along reclaimed riverbank, originally as a geisha district. The name comes from the Portuguese "ponto" (point). Today it's lined with hundreds of restaurants from kaiseki splurges to standing bars. In summer (May–September) many restaurants build kawayuka wooden platforms over the river for outdoor dining.

👀 Look for
  • The chidori bird crest — small plover symbol marking traditional Pontocho establishments
  • Numbered lanterns — each restaurant has a number on its lantern
  • The Kamogawa riverbank just below — peak sakura in season, people sitting equidistantly apart (the famous "Kamogawa equal-spacing" phenomenon)
🍜 Eat here

For family-friendly: Kyoto Pontocho Robata (open grill, fun for kids), Pontocho Misoguigawa (French-Japanese fusion). Most places require reservations — book 2+ weeks ahead for sakura week. Yagenbori is a more affordable izakaya option.

📸 Photo spot

Looking down the alley from the south entrance just after sunset, lanterns glowing. The Kamogawa river at sakura time, from Sanjo Bridge.

⏱ Dinner (1.5–2 hrs) 💰 ¥4000–15000+/person 🚇 Sanjo Stn 📅 Reserve ahead!

Day 8 · Iconic Kyoto

Fushimi Inari & Tofuku-ji

Loading…

Fushimi Inari Shrine 伏見稲荷大社

10,000 orange torii gates climbing a sacred mountain — Japan's most photographed shrine and the symbol of Kyoto.

📖 The story

Founded in 711 AD, this is the head shrine of all 30,000 Inari shrines in Japan. Inari is the Shinto god of rice, sake, prosperity, and (by extension) business. Each torii gate was donated by an individual or business — small ones cost ~¥400,000 ($3,000), large ones up to ¥1.3 million ($10,000+). New gates are added constantly as old ones decay.

The white fox statues are kitsune — messengers of Inari, often shown holding keys to rice granaries or jewels in their mouths.

👀 What to do
  • Climb to the Yotsutsuji intersection (about 45 min up) — view of all Kyoto
  • Full summit loop is 4km / 2–3 hrs round trip — surprisingly doable for kids
  • Senbon Torii (Thousand Torii Gates) — the most iconic tunnel section, near the bottom
  • Side trails to small fox shrines hidden in the forest
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The orange tunnel is genuinely magical. Make it a hike challenge — climb to Yotsutsuji and they earn a snack at the summit tea house. Buy a fox-face cookie (kitsune senbei) at one of the stalls near the entrance.

🍜 Eat here

The street leading to the shrine has stalls selling inari-zushi (rice in sweet fried tofu pouches — named after the shrine) and kitsune udon (fox noodles — udon with fried tofu, named the same way). Vermillion Café at the summit is a hidden gem with rare bamboo-grove views.

📸 Photo spot

Most tourists stop at Senbon Torii (Thousand Gates) and turn around — meaning that 15 minutes further up the trail you'll have empty tunnels mostly to yourself.

⏱ 2–4 hrs 💰 Free 🚇 JR Inari Stn ⏰ Arrive 7am or after 5pm

Tōfuku-ji 東福寺

One of Kyoto's "Five Great Zen Temples" — famous for its modernist rock gardens and the dramatic Tsutenkyo Bridge.

📖 The story

Founded in 1236, deliberately named to invoke both Tōdaiji (the Buddha temple in Nara) and Kōfuku-ji (also in Nara). The compound's design draws from both. The current Hojo (abbot's hall) gardens were designed in 1939 by Mirei Shigemori — radical for fusing traditional Japanese garden elements with modernist geometry. The four gardens represent the four seasons.

👀 Look for
  • Tsutenkyo Bridge ("Bridge to Heaven") — covered wooden bridge over a maple-filled ravine
  • The four Hojo gardens — north garden has a famous checkerboard moss pattern
  • Sanmon Gate — Japan's oldest standing Zen temple gate (1425), 22m tall
🍁 Off-peak gem

Famous in autumn (maple foliage) but mostly empty during sakura season — perfect quiet stop on the way back from Fushimi Inari (one train stop away).

⏱ 1 hr 💰 ¥500/adult 🚇 Tōfukuji Stn

Arashiyama

Arashiyama Bamboo Grove 嵐山竹林

A 500-meter walk through 50-meter-tall moso bamboo — listed by the Japanese government as one of "100 Soundscapes of Japan" for the way wind makes the forest creak and sing.

📖 The story

The bamboo here is the moso species, which can grow up to 1 meter per day during the growing season. Some shoots emerge from the ground and reach full 25m height in just 90 days. The grove has been cultivated for centuries — bamboo from Arashiyama traditionally supplies the highest-quality tea ceremony utensils and chopsticks.

👀 Tips

Tenryū-ji 天龍寺

A UNESCO World Heritage Zen temple with what is widely considered Japan's oldest unchanged garden — designed 700 years ago and still in its original layout.

📖 The story

Built in 1339 by shogun Ashikaga Takauji to honor Emperor Go-Daigo, his former enemy. The funds came from a single trading mission to China — a fascinating piece of medieval diplomacy. The Sogenchi Garden uses Mt. Arashiyama as shakkei ("borrowed scenery"), where the surrounding landscape becomes part of the composition. The garden has never been redesigned since 1345.

Iwatayama Monkey Park 嵐山モンキーパーク

A 20-minute uphill hike to a hilltop sanctuary where 120 wild Japanese macaques roam free — and humans stand inside a cage to feed them.

📖 The story

The macaques here are wild — researchers started feeding them in 1956 to study their behavior, and they've stayed connected to the park ever since. Day 10 · Day Trip

Nara — deer + giant Buddha

Loading…

Nara Park 奈良公園

Japan's first permanent capital (710 AD) — home to 1,200 free-roaming sacred deer that have learned to bow for crackers.

📖 The story

In 710 AD the Imperial Court moved here from Asuka, making Nara Japan's first true capital city. Buddhism became the state religion under Emperor Shomu, and Nara became a major center of culture. After only 74 years, the capital moved to Kyoto (in part because the Buddhist clergy had become too politically powerful), but the temples remained.

The deer are considered sacred in Shinto — messengers of the gods. Until 1637, killing a deer in Nara was punishable by death. Today they're a protected species, technically wild, and have evolved to bow to humans for treats.

👀 What to do
  • Buy shika-senbei (deer crackers) from a vendor — ¥200/pack, sold throughout the park
  • Visit Todai-ji for the giant Buddha (separate listing below)
  • Kasuga Taisha — shrine with 3,000 stone lanterns lining its paths
  • Mt. Wakakusa — grassy hill, easy 30 min hike, panoramic view
  • Naramachi — old merchant district, beautifully preserved machiya houses
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Buy crackers one pack at a time and feed quickly — the deer can be aggressive once they smell food. Some will headbutt or chase you. Don't hide crackers in pockets — they will find them. If a child is scared, pick them up.

🍜 Eat here

Nakatanidou (mochi pounding) — they pound mochi by hand with a giant wooden mallet at terrifying speed (every ~30 min throughout the day, free to watch). Get the yomogi mochi after. For lunch: Tsukihitei deep in the forest, ¥10000+ kaiseki splurge, or casual: Ten Ten Café.

⏱ Half day from Kyoto 💰 Park free; temples ¥600/each 🚇 45 min by JR from Kyoto

Tōdai-ji & the Great Buddha 東大寺・大仏

One of the world's largest wooden buildings, housing a 15-meter, 500-ton bronze Buddha cast in 752 AD.

📖 The story

Emperor Shomu ordered Todai-ji built in 738 after a smallpox epidemic — the Buddha was supposed to bring peace and protection to the realm. Casting the bronze Buddha took 2.6 million people working 8 years; nearly all of Japan's copper supply went into it. The 50,000-person dedication ceremony in 752 was one of the largest gatherings in pre-modern Japanese history.

The Daibutsuden (Great Buddha Hall) has been destroyed twice by fire. The current hall, completed in 1709, is two-thirds the size of the original — and still the largest wooden building in the world.

👀 Look for
  • The Buddha's bronze lotus petals — engraved with detailed cosmologies
  • Two giant Niō (guardian) statues at the Nandaimon gate — 8.4m tall, carved 1203 by Unkei
  • The pillar with a hole at the back-right of the Daibutsuden — same size as the Buddha's nostril; crawling through is said to grant enlightenment in the next life
  • The Bishamonten and Komokuten guardian statues flanking the Buddha
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The Buddha's nostril hole is a famous kid challenge — your 9-year-old will likely fit easily; the 11-year-old can probably squeeze through; the 12-year-old may not. There's almost always a line. Going through head-first, arms out.

⏱ 1.5 hrs 💰 ¥600/adult 🚇 Walk from Nara Park

Day 11 · North Kyoto

Golden Pavilion & Zen gardens

Loading…

Kinkaku-ji (Golden Pavilion) 金閣寺

A three-story Zen temple covered top to bottom in gold leaf, mirrored in a perfectly still pond.

📖 The story

Originally built in 1397 as a retirement villa for shogun Ashikaga Yoshimitsu — converted into a Zen temple after his death. Each of the three floors is in a different architectural style: Shinden (palace) on bottom, Samurai-zukuri (warrior) in middle, Karayō (Chinese Zen) on top — symbolizing the unification of those three forces under Yoshimitsu.

In 1950 a deranged young monk named Hayashi Yoken set fire to the pavilion and destroyed it. He was sentenced to 7 years; his suicide attempt and the case were the basis for Yukio Mishima's novel The Temple of the Golden Pavilion (1956). The temple was rebuilt 1955; the gold leaf was completely re-applied in 1987.

👀 Look for
  • The phoenix statue on top of the pavilion
  • The Mirror Pond (Kyoko-chi) — designed specifically to reflect the pavilion at all heights
  • Small "good luck" stone tablets tourists throw coins at on the path
  • Sekka-tei tea house at the top of the garden path — usually overlooked
📸 Photo spot

The first view as you turn the corner from the entrance is THE shot. Morning light makes the gold luminous. The path is one-way — no return for a second look. Bring a polarizing filter if you have one for the reflection.

⏱ 45 min 💰 ¥500/adult 🚇 Bus 101/205 ⏰ Go early morning

Loading…

Ryōan-ji 龍安寺

The most famous Zen rock garden in the world: 15 stones, raked white gravel, and a puzzle no one has solved.

📖 The story

The temple dates to 1450; the garden was created around 1500. The designer is unknown. The garden is just 248 square meters: 15 stones arranged in five groups (5-2-3-2-3) in white gravel raked daily by monks. From any angle you can see at most 14 of the 15 stones. To see all 15, the legend says, you must achieve enlightenment.

The meaning is fiercely debated. The official Zen line: "tora-no-ko-watashi" (tiger crossing — a mother tiger and cubs crossing a river). Other theories: the stones represent the islands of Japan, or major stars, or simply the impossibility of perfect knowledge. A 2002 mathematical study found that the stones map to a subtle "axis of symmetry" perceptible only subconsciously.

👀 How to experience it
  • Sit on the wooden veranda — this is the only authorized viewing angle
  • Try to count all 15 from where you sit — you won't
  • Move slowly along the veranda; the composition shifts
  • Visit before 9am — by 10am, busloads arrive, ruining the silence
⏱ 45 min 💰 ¥600/adult 🚇 10 min walk from Kinkaku-ji

Nijō Castle 二条城

A 17th-century shogun's palace famous for its "nightingale floors" — wooden boards that chirp when walked on, an anti-assassin alarm system.

📖 The story

Built 1603 as the Kyoto residence of Tokugawa Ieyasu, founder of the Tokugawa shogunate that ruled Japan for 265 years. Symbolically: the castle was built bigger than the Imperial Palace next door — sending an unmissable message about where real power lay. Ironically, it was also here in 1867 that the 15th and last Tokugawa shogun returned power to the Emperor, ending the era.

👀 Don't miss
  • Ninomaru Palace — 33 rooms with painted screens by the Kano school (you can walk through)
  • Nightingale floors — the floor nails are designed to squeak with movement; impossible to walk silently
  • Throne Room (Ohiroma) — where the shogun met daimyo lords; you can see the ceremonial arrangement
  • Ninomaru Garden — by master gardener Kobori Enshu
  • 400+ cherry trees including late-blooming weeping varieties (yae-zakura)
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The squeaking floors are a hit. Tell kids about ninja before going — they'll have fun trying (and failing) to walk silently. Castle is open to walk through interior — you'll go in your socks.

Higashiyama & Philosopher's Path

Philosopher's Path 哲学の道

A 2km stone path along a canal lined with 400 cherry trees — the postcard image of Kyoto in sakura season.

📖 The story

Named after philosopher Nishida Kitarō (1870–1945), founder of the influential Kyoto School of philosophy, who walked this path daily on his commute to Kyoto University, using the walk to meditate. The canal itself is part of the Lake Biwa Canal system, an 1890 engineering marvel that brought water and hydroelectric power 30km from Lake Biwa.

The 400 cherry trees were planted in 1922 by Japanese painter Hashimoto Kansetsu.

👀 Along the way
  • Ginkaku-ji (Silver Pavilion) at the north end — never actually got its silver coating
  • Honen-in temple — small, free, almost always empty
  • Nyakuoji Shrine — quiet forest shrine
  • Tiny cafes and craft shops along side streets
📸 Photo spot

In peak sakura, the entire path becomes a tunnel of pink reflected in the canal. Walk south-to-north (from Eikando temple to Ginkaku-ji). On windy days, falling petals coat the water — pink rivers.

Higashiyama District 東山

The most preserved old quarter of Kyoto — sloped cobblestone lanes lined with Edo-period wooden machiya houses, tea shops, and crafts.

📖 The story

The streets Ninen-zaka ("two-year hill") and Sannen-zaka ("three-year hill") are over 1,000 years old, dating to the founding of nearby Kiyomizu-dera in 798 AD. The names come from old superstitions: if you trip on Ninen-zaka, you'll have 2 years of bad luck; on Sannen-zaka, 3 years. (Whether the steep slope and slippery cobblestones make this a real risk is up for debate.)

👀 Walking route
  • Start at Yasaka Pagoda (Hokan-ji) — 5-story pagoda from 1440, iconic photo
  • Walk up Yasaka-dori with the pagoda framed at the end
  • Turn into Ninen-zaka → Sannen-zaka — cobblestone, shops
  • End at Kiyomizu-dera at the top
  • Best shops: Ippodo Tea (300-year-old tea house), Yatsuhashi (cinnamon mochi sweets), Studio Ghibli store, Snoopy Tea House
🍜 Eat here

Many small craft cafes — % Arabica Kyoto for excellent coffee, Saryo Tsujiri for matcha parfaits. Try yatsuhashi (Kyoto's signature sweet — cinnamon-rice-flour triangles, traditional or filled with bean paste).

⏱ 2 hrs 💰 Free walk; shops vary 🚇 Bus to Kiyomizu-dera 🛍 Best souvenir shopping

Kiyomizu-dera 清水寺

UNESCO temple perched on a 13-meter wooden stage built without nails — sweeping views over Kyoto.

📖 The story

Founded in 778 AD by a monk who saw a vision near the Otowa waterfall. The name means "Pure Water Temple" after that waterfall. The main hall's wooden veranda juts out 13m over the hillside, supported by 78 wooden pillars driven into the slope — built in 1633, the entire structure uses zero nails. Until 1872, an old custom held that if you jumped off the stage and survived, your wish would come true (234 documented jumps, 85% survival rate, now strictly forbidden).

👀 Don't miss
  • The main stage — view over all of Kyoto
  • Jishu Shrine (behind the main hall) — has two "love stones" 18m apart; walk from one to the other with eyes closed for success in love. If you need help, it means you need help in love too.
  • Otowa Waterfall — three streams; drink from one for longevity, success in love, or success in academics. Drinking from all three is greedy and brings no luck.
  • Tainai-meguri (₹100) — totally dark underground passage representing Buddha's womb; navigate by holding a rope
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

The dark Tainai-meguri passage is creepy in a fun way — kids love it. Hold the rope, walk in pitch black through a series of small chambers, find a single lit stone, make a wish, exit.

📸 Photo spot

Visit late afternoon — climb the path from Higashiyama, see the main hall as the sun sets over Kyoto. The temple often does evening illuminations during sakura season.

Osaka Castle 大阪城

Originally built in 1583 by warlord Toyotomi Hideyoshi as his power base to unify Japan — destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, now a museum at the center of one of Osaka's largest parks.

📖 The story

This castle is the symbol of one of Japanese history's most dramatic eras. Toyotomi Hideyoshi, a peasant who rose to rule Japan, built it in 1583 to outshine all other castles. After his death in 1598, his clan was crushed by Tokugawa Ieyasu in the legendary Siege of Osaka (1614–1615) — the battle that consolidated Tokugawa rule for 250 years.

The castle has burned and been rebuilt repeatedly. The current main tower is a 1931 reconstruction in concrete — with an elevator. The interior is an excellent modern museum about Hideyoshi and the unification of Japan: original armor, screens, holograms, and observation deck on top.

👀 Don't miss
  • The museum's 8th floor observation deck — best Osaka view
  • Hideyoshi's golden tea room reconstruction inside
  • 3,000 cherry trees in the surrounding park — Osaka's top hanami spot
  • Plum grove (Bairin) — east side of the castle, 1,200 plum trees
  • The Otemon gate — original 1628 structure, hand-cut massive stones
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

Inside the museum, kids can try on samurai armor and kimono for free (limited times per day) — great photo op. The castle's holograms and dioramas of the siege are kid-friendly.

Dotonbori 道頓堀

800m of canal-side neon, giant moving food signs, and street food — Osaka's most overwhelmingly fun strip.

📖 The story

The Dotonbori canal was dug in 1612 by an entrepreneur named Yasui Doton, trying to make the area navigable. He died before finishing it — his cousins completed the work and named the canal after him. By the 1660s, the area was already a major entertainment district.

The famous Glico Running Man billboard has been here since 1935 — Japan's longest-running outdoor advertisement. The Kani Doraku giant moving crab sign dates to 1960.

👀 Iconic signs
  • Glico Running Man — at the Ebisu Bridge; everyone takes the same photo
  • Kani Doraku giant moving crab — 6m wide, claws move
  • Kuidaore Taro — drum-beating clown statue, the unofficial mascot of Osaka
  • Don Quijote Ferris wheel — yes, there's a Ferris wheel on the side of a discount store
  • Tomato pig, giant gyoza, giant takoyaki — every restaurant has its own 3D billboard
🍜 Must eat (street food crawl style)
  • Takoyaki (octopus balls) — invented in Osaka in 1935. Try Wanaka or Kukuru
  • Okonomiyaki (savory pancake) — try Mizuno (since 1945) on Hozenji Yokocho
  • Kushikatsu (fried skewers) — try Daruma. RULE: no double-dipping the sauce
  • Horumon grilled offal at the small smoky places off the main strip
  • Pablo cheesecake — for dessert, started in Osaka
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

This is a sensory overload zone — kids will love it but might get tired. Plan it as an evening, eat a bunch of small things rather than one sit-down meal. Try the takoyaki — Osakans take this seriously; even a casual one will be the best they've had.

Kuromon Ichiba Market 黒門市場

"Osaka's kitchen" — 600m of covered indoor stalls selling fresh seafood, wagyu, fruit, and street food, with 170 years of history.

Named after a black gate (kuromon) of a now-destroyed temple. Started as a fish market in the late Edo period (mid-1800s) — the area was perfect for fishermen coming up from Osaka Bay. Day 14 · Day Trip

Himeji Castle

Loading…

Himeji Castle 姫路城

"The White Heron Castle" — Japan's most beautiful, best-preserved feudal castle, never destroyed by war or earthquake.

📖 The story

The site has been fortified since 1333; the current complex was completed in 1609 under Ikeda Terumasa, son-in-law of Tokugawa Ieyasu. 83 buildings survive: keep, gates, walls, defensive courtyards. The brilliant white exterior comes from special plaster — visually beautiful, and also fireproof, a 17th-century innovation.

The castle's nickname "White Heron" (Hakuro-jo) comes from the way the white walls and curved roofs resemble a heron taking flight. Survived WW2 bombings (the city around it was destroyed) and the 1995 Kobe earthquake. Underwent a 5-year, $50M restoration completed 2015 that restored the brilliant white plaster to its original look.

👀 Why it's the best castle
  • It's the original — unlike most "castles" in Japan, which are concrete 20th-century reconstructions
  • Defensive maze design — the path to the keep zigzags through 21 gates and multiple courtyards designed to confuse attackers; you'll experience this walking up
  • Six floors of original wooden interiors — climb to the top, see weapons racks, defensive holes, hidden trapdoors
  • "Stone-dropping holes" — Western castles had murder holes; Japanese castles dropped boiling oil and rocks
  • Cherry trees ring the castle — the iconic shot is the castle behind a foreground of sakura
👨‍👩‍👧 Kid tip

This is a REAL samurai castle, not a reconstruction — your boys will appreciate the difference. The climb to the top floor is steep wooden stairs, increasingly narrow. They'll find spear racks, gun ports, evidence of the defensive design at every floor.

📸 Photo spot

The Sangoku-bori moat with cherry trees in front and the keep behind. From Himeji Station, the castle is visible at the end of a 1km tree-lined street — that's the postcard.

⏱ Half day from Osaka 💰 ¥1000/adult, ¥300/child 🚇 Shinkansen 30 min from Osaka 🌸 Castle + sakura = iconic

  • Honke Owariya — Soba since 1465. That's 560 years. Their signature "Hourai soba" comes in 5 stacked lacquered boxes with rotating toppings (egg, shiitake, daikon, sesame, nori). ¥1,500–2,200.
  • Omen (Gion) — Hand-pulled udon. You assemble each bowl yourself with toppings (ginger, sesame, scallions) at the table. ¥1,500.
⏱ Market 2 hr, restaurants 1 hr 💰 ¥3,000–10,000 for 5

Osaka: The street-food trinity 大阪粉もん

Osaka's philosophy is kuidaore — "eat until you collapse." Hit all three on a single Dotonbori night.

3 worth seeking out
  • Mizuno (Dotonbori) — Okonomiyaki since 1945. Their yamaimo-yaki is the signature — uses grated mountain yam instead of flour, yields almost-soufflé texture. ¥1,500–2,200. Cash only, often a queue, no reservations.
  • Wanaka Sennichimae (near Namba) — Takoyaki (octopus balls). Crispy outside, molten lava inside. 8 balls ¥600. Eat hot but careful — they're 200°F (95°C) in the center; locals blow on each ball methodically.
  • Daruma (Shinsekai) — Kushikatsu (deep-fried skewers — meat, vegetables, mochi, anything). One communal sauce trough per table; ABSOLUTELY no double-dipping (signs on every wall, ¥10,000 fine). 10 sticks ¥1,500.
⏱ Crawl 2–3 hrs 💰 ¥15,000 for 5 (3 stops) 💵 Bring cash

Underestimate at your peril

Convenience stores コンビニ

Japanese conbini are genuinely great food — Michelin chefs eat at them. Breakfast for 5: ¥2,000–2,500 ($14–18).

What each chain is famous for
  • 7-Eleven — The legendary egg sandwich (Anthony Bourdain called it "unnaturally good"). Best onigiri (tuna mayo, salmon, ume plum). Premium puddings.
  • Family Mart — "Famichiki" fried chicken at the counter (¥230 — your kids will ask for seconds). Best hot foods overall.
  • Lawson — Karaage-kun (bite-size boneless fried chicken, ¥260). Best premium desserts and roll cakes. Best fresh-baked bread in the morning.

All have hot food counters, fresh sandwiches changed twice daily, ATMs, free WiFi, beer/wine. Open 24/7. Use them for breakfast and shinkansen snacks.

Department store basements デパ地下

"Depachika" — the food-hall floor of a major department store. Restaurant-grade prepared food sold by the gram. Family dinner of 5: ¥5,000–8,000.

3 worth seeking out
  • Daimaru Tokyo (Tokyo Station, B1) — Hundreds of stalls. Crucially: pick up your ekiben (train bento) here on the way to the shinkansen platform.
  • Isetan Shinjuku (B1) — The most famous depachika in Japan. Cake and patisserie displays look like jewelry stores. Pricier but spectacular.
  • Daimaru Umeda (Osaka, B1) — Strategy: after 7pm, prepared foods go on 30–50% discount as they near sell-by time. Show up at 7:30pm for half-price sushi platters.

Ekiben — bento for the train

Ekiben 駅弁

"Station bento" — a 100-year tradition of beautifully-packaged regional bento boxes sold at major train stations. ¥1,200–2,500 each.

Where to buy
  • GranSta Tokyo (Tokyo Station B1) — The flagship ekiben hall. 100+ varieties from across Japan. Buy here before catching the shinkansen to Kyoto.
  • Kyoto Station Isetan B1 — Smaller selection but excellent Kyoto-style boxes. Catch the train back to Osaka or Tokyo with one in hand.
  • Shin-Osaka Station ekiben counters — Best Osaka specialties for the way home, including takoyaki bento.
3 most famous ekiben
  • Daruma-bento (Gunma) — Comes packaged in a red plastic tanuki raccoon-dog figurine; kids keep the figurine. ¥1,250.
  • Yonezawa Gyu Niku-don (Yamagata) — Premium wagyu beef on rice, served warm thanks to a self-heating string mechanism. Pull the string and the bento heats itself in 5 minutes. ¥1,500.
  • Anago Meshi (Hiroshima) — Saltwater eel over rice; available at Tokyo Station. ¥1,800.

Etiquette: eating on shinkansen is normal and expected. Eating on commuter trains is not — wait for the platform or station.
